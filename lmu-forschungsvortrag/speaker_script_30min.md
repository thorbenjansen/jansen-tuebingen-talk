## Delivery Principle

The talk should not sound like a list of projects. The spine is:

I build and study effective teaching and learning arrangements for argumentation. The recurring problem is that argumentation is important but difficult to teach, assess, and support in real time. My contribution is to combine learning theory, formative assessment, automated analysis, motivation research, and scalable teacher support into a coherent program of use-inspired basic research.

The logic is: start from theory and target-group needs, build interventions, test mechanisms under increasingly realistic conditions, scale the work, and return to theory development.

## Slide 1. Effective Argumentation Instruction Across Science and Language Education

Target time: 0:30

Thank you very much for the opportunity to present my work. My talk is about effective argumentation instruction across science and language education. The central idea is that students learn argumentation best when we can make their thinking visible while it is still developing. Pedagogical AI is interesting here not as a replacement for teachers, but as a way to support formative assessment, student motivation, and learning in the process.

Transition: I start with the educational problem that motivates the program.

## Slide 2. Starting Point

Target time: 0:55

Argumentative competencies are central for participation in school and society. Students need to build positions, justify them with evidence, communicate them clearly, and revise them when they encounter better reasons. But argumentation is not one simple skill. In science education, students coordinate claims, evidence, models, and domain knowledge. In language education, they also organize a text, formulate a position, and write in an appropriate register.

That creates a practical teaching problem. Effective argumentation instruction requires continuous diagnosis and feedback while students are working. Teachers need to see what students understand, where an argument breaks down, and what kind of support would help next.

Transition: This connects to a broader framework of teaching quality.

## Slide 3. Supporting Teachers' Formative Assessment, Student Motivation, and Learning with Pedagogical AI

Target time: 0:50

I locate my work in teaching-quality research, especially cognitive activation, student support, and classroom management. In argumentation, cognitive activation means prompting students to reason with claims, evidence, and quality criteria rather than only stating opinions. Student support means that feedback arrives while students can still use it. Classroom management means organizing complex environments where students write, revise, use tools, and receive guidance.

The phrase "pedagogical AI" is important. I am not interested in AI as a generic technology. I am interested in AI when it is embedded in teaching arrangements that improve diagnosis, feedback, and learning.

Transition: The structure of the talk follows this development logic.

## Slide 4. Structure of the Talk

Target time: 1:10

I structure the talk as use-inspired basic research. I start with target-group needs. Then I develop the intervention basis: formative assessment, learning progressions, motivation theory, and automated assessment. Next, I show pilot evidence. Then I move to efficacy studies, where the focus is internal validity: under controlled conditions, does the mechanism work? After that, I move to effectiveness studies, where the focus is external validity: how does the mechanism work under realistic instructional conditions?

Finally, I show how the work scales into professional development, grant acquisition, current PISA research, and theory development. So the aim is not only to test tools. The aim is to build evidence-based teaching and learning arrangements and use them to advance theory.

Transition: First, what are the needs of teachers and students?

## Slide 5. Teachers' Professional Competencies

Target time: 0:30

The first part maps target-group needs. I use three evidence sources: work on teachers' professional competencies, large-scale studies of argumentative competencies, and meta-analyses on achievement, motivation, and self-beliefs. I begin with teachers because their assessment competence is central for formative support.

Transition: The next slide unpacks teacher judgment.

## Slide 6. Teachers' Professional Competencies

Target time: 0:55

This work connects to roughly 40 years of teacher judgment accuracy research. The central idea is to compare teachers' judgments with students' criterion-based performance and ask where judgments are accurate, where they are biased, and under which conditions they improve.

Our contribution was to bring this logic into argumentative writing. That is demanding because teachers do not simply "see" argumentative competence. Their judgments are shaped by teacher characteristics, student characteristics, task features, and available evidence. Argument quality combines content, structure, evidence, reasoning, and language. If assessment is noisy or biased, feedback will also be noisy or biased.

Transition: The second need concerns students' actual argumentative competencies.

## Slide 7. Large-Scale Studies About Argumentative Competencies

Target time: 0:55

Here I show two empirical foundations. In science, we analyzed 4,589 essays from 1,839 secondary students. In foreign-language argumentation, we worked with 2,314 TOEFL argumentative essays from year 11 students in Germany and Switzerland.

Action: briefly click the science task and foreign-language task cards.

These popups show why the tasks matter. Argumentation has to be validated in the tasks students actually write. The studies gave us corpora, rating criteria, and evidence about where students struggle: connecting claims to evidence, organizing reasoning, and producing arguments that are convincing within a specific subject.

Transition: The third evidence source broadens the view from performance to motivation and self-beliefs.

## Slide 8. Meta-Analyses About Learning-Relevant Outcomes

Target time: 0:35

These meta-analyses are important because intervention research should not focus only on achievement. Learning is also shaped by motivation and self-beliefs. One implication became central for this program: students' motivation and self-beliefs are strongly connected to whether they experience that they are learning. They need to see that their actions can improve competence.

For this talk, the implication is: feedback and AI support should be evaluated not only by whether a text improves, but also by whether students remain engaged, feel competent, and learn to regulate their writing.

Transition: This leads to the first interim conclusion.

## Slide 9. Target-Group Needs Define the Intervention

Target time: 0:35

The conclusion from the needs section is simple: target-group needs must define the intervention. Teachers are challenged to assess complex argumentation. Students are challenged to produce arguments that are coherent, evidence-based, and subject-specific. The risk is to design digital tools from a technology perspective rather than from an evidence-based needs perspective.

My contribution is to map these needs empirically and use them to build support for teachers and students.

Transition: The next section explains how this becomes intervention development.

## Slide 10. Theoretical and Method Basis

Target time: 0:30

The development section has two foundations. The theoretical basis is formative assessment and learning progressions for argumentation. The methodological basis is automated assessment, because scalable feedback requires models that can analyze student texts with sufficient quality.

Transition: I start with formative assessment.

## Slide 11. Formative Assessment

Target time: 0:35

Formative assessment is built around three student questions: Where am I going? Where am I now? And what is the next step? In feedback terms, this corresponds to feed-up, feedback, and feed-forward. For argumentation instruction, this is crucial because students need more than a score. They need to understand the goal, locate their current argument, and receive guidance for revision.

Transition: Students also need a model of how argumentation becomes more sophisticated.

## Slide 12. Situated Expectancy-Value Theory

Target time: 0:40

The motivation side is captured by situated expectancy-value theory. In each learning situation, students ask two simple questions: Can I do this task? And do I want to do this task? If both answers are yes, students act.

This matters for AI feedback because feedback can change both sides. It can support expectancy by making progress visible and manageable. It can support value by showing why revision is worthwhile. But it can also fail if students do not trust it, do not understand it, or do not see how to use it.

Transition: The learning progression specifies what students are trying to do.

## Slide 13. Argumentation Support Becomes More Sophisticated Across Three Steps

Target time: 1:25

This slide shows the learning progression that organizes the intervention logic. First, students understand the task and build an initial position. Second, they build the argument by connecting claims, evidence, and reasoning. Third, they refine the argument by testing it, defending it, evaluating alternatives, and revising.

Action: click the three boxes if you want to show the detail popups.

In the first popup, the energy task illustrates that even understanding the task is not trivial. Students have to compare options and criteria. In the second popup, the position becomes an argument: claim, evidence, warrant, and eventually counterclaim and rebuttal. In the third popup, students refine through interaction and revision. The point is that feedback should not be generic. A student who has not understood the task needs different support from a student who already needs counterarguments.

Transition: To support this at scale, we need reliable assessment data.

## Slide 14. Human Expert Ratings

Target time: 0:55

This slide shows the empirical basis for automated assessment. In science argumentation, we used learning-progression-based ratings that capture content and structure at the sentence level. In foreign-language argumentation, we used analytical and holistic expert ratings for content, style, organization, and language quality, in cooperation with Educational Testing Service.

Action: click the science expert ratings card.

The popup makes the methodological point concrete: AI models are trained against high-quality human expert ratings. We do not ask the model to invent what quality means. We first define quality through theory and expert judgment, then test whether automated models reproduce those judgments with sufficient reliability.

Transition: This leads to the development-section conclusion.

## Slide 15. AI Can Accurately Simulate Human Assessment of Argumentation

Target time: 0:30

The conclusion is that AI can accurately simulate human assessment of argumentation when the criteria are theoretically grounded and the human ratings are strong. This makes formative assessment scalable. It allows us to move from delayed summative correction to process-oriented feedback while students are still writing.

Transition: The next question is whether this support works in practice.

## Slide 16. Pilot Study

Target time: 0:30

The pilot section tests the mechanism of action on both sides of the instructional process. On the teacher side, the question is whether tools can train assessment competencies by helping teachers focus on relevant features of student arguments. On the student side, the question is whether AI-based feedback supports revision and learning.

Transition: I start with the feedback mechanism.

## Slide 17. Feedback Worked Best as a Full Cycle

Target time: 1:15

In the student-side pilot work, we varied feedback following Hattie and Timperley's feedback model. The model distinguishes three questions: feed-up, feedback, and feed-forward. Feed-up clarifies the target. Feedback locates the current state. Feed-forward gives the next step.

Action: click the feedback screenshot and scroll through it.

The enlarged screenshot shows these three parts in a concrete feedback environment. At the top, students receive information about the target. In the middle, they receive information about their current performance. At the bottom, they receive actionable checkpoints for revision. The key result is that isolated feedback is not enough. Students benefit most when all three questions are answered together.

Transition: The teacher-side pilot work focuses on assessment competence.

## Slide 18. Efficacy Study

Target time: 0:25

Here the talk moves from the pilot mechanism into efficacy work. The teacher-side line asks whether assessment competence can be trained. The student-side line asks whether pedagogical AI feedback effects hold under more controlled conditions.

Transition: First, the teacher-side tool.

## Slide 19. Training Teachers' Assessment Competencies

Target time: 0:45

The TRACE Tool is a teacher assessment simulation. Teachers practice assessment decisions on student argumentation, receive structured cases, and can compare their judgments to expert-based criteria.

Action: click the TRACE Tool screenshot.

The mechanism is diagnostic focus. The tool makes relevant assessment points in the text salient, for example whether there is a claim, whether evidence supports it, and whether reasoning connects both. At the same time, less relevant cues should become less dominant. This is deliberate practice for assessment.

Transition: On the student side, the question is whether AI feedback improves revision.

## Slide 20. AI-Based Feedback Supports Revision

Target time: 0:45

Across pilot and efficacy work, AI-based feedback supports revision. It improves revision performance, works better than no external feedback, and in some conditions can approach the effects of teacher feedback. This does not mean that AI replaces teacher feedback. It means that feedback generated from automated assessment can become additional support when teachers cannot provide immediate individual feedback to every student.

Transition: But the evidence also shows a challenge.

## Slide 21. AI Feedback Does Not Help All Students Equally

Target time: 0:45

The challenge is that AI feedback does not help all students equally. In one large context, about half of 20,000 students did not use AI feedback. Even when feedback is available, effects are distributed unequally. Some students engage deeply, revise strategically, and benefit. Others ignore the feedback, misunderstand it, or do not transform it into revision.

So the question becomes: for which students, through which mechanisms, and under which instructional conditions does AI feedback work?

Transition: This leads to the effectiveness phase.

## Slide 22. Effectiveness Study

Target time: 0:30

The effectiveness phase moves from internal validity to external validity. It asks how the mechanisms work under more realistic instructional conditions. On the teacher side, this is TRACE-G. On the student side, it is the feedback experiment with secondary students.

## Slide 25. Research Question

Target time: 0:30

The research question is: Which student characteristics moderate the effect of AI feedback? The condition is AI feedback versus self-feedback. The outcomes are writing quality and motivation. Student characteristics are the moderators.

This matters because equal access to AI feedback does not guarantee equal benefit.

Transition: The procedure allows us to inspect the process.

## Slide 26. Study Procedure

Target time: 0:45

Students write, receive support, revise, and answer questionnaires. The design treats writing as a process. We are not only comparing final texts. We examine what happens between first draft, feedback, revision, and student perception.

Action: click the writing workspace screenshot.

The popup shows the environment students worked in: task materials, writing area, feedback, and revision space. This matters because the intervention is not just a message. It is an arrangement that shapes attention, revision, and engagement.

Transition: The first result concerns engagement during revision.

## Slide 27. AI Feedback Increases Engagement During Text Revision

Target time: 0:40

The main effect is that AI feedback increases engagement during text revision. Students who receive generative-AI feedback engage more with argumentative elements during revision. This is educationally meaningful because revision is often the moment where feedback becomes learning. If students do not engage, feedback remains external information. If they engage, feedback can shape attention, strategy, and improvement.

Transition: The moderation result explains for whom this works.

## Slide 28. Student Characteristics Moderate the Effect of AI Feedback via Engagement

Target time: 1:20

The central result is that student characteristics moderate the effect of AI feedback through engagement. Feedback does not act directly on learning in a simple way. Students first have to perceive it as useful, engage with it, and transform it into revision behavior. Their receptivity, motivation, and background influence this process.

Action: click the story model and scroll slowly from top to bottom.

Explain the model as a process: feedback quality shapes perceived usefulness. Receptivity, motivation, and background come in as student-side moderators. Perceived usefulness connects to affective and behavioral engagement. These engagement processes then link to motivation, revision performance, and self-beliefs. This is the conceptual bridge between feedback research and motivation research.

Transition: The interim conclusion is that feedback must become learning support.

## Slide 29. Feedback Has to Become Learning Support

Target time: 0:35

The conclusion is that AI feedback can increase revision performance, but feedback alone is not enough. Feedback has to become learning support. That means students need to understand the target, locate their current performance, and know the next step. It also means that all students should benefit, not only those who already have high receptivity or strong self-regulation.

## Slide 36. Theory

Target time: 1:10

The theoretical contribution has three parts. First, the work integrates AI into motivation and self-belief formation. Students compare themselves not only with peers or standards, but increasingly with AI-supported performances and AI-generated suggestions. Second, the work expands argumentation learning progressions with AI support. Students can build, inspect, and revise arguments with AI as part of the learning environment. Third, it expands formative assessment theory through process feedback.

## Slide 37. Students Use Generative AI While Writing

Target time: 0:25

The current challenge is that students already use generative AI while writing. They ask for ideas, formulations, structure, and revision support. This changes the learning environment and creates a new assessment challenge. We need to understand what students actually do with AI and how this changes argumentation, motivation, and learning.

## Slide 38. PISA Gives a Controlled Window Into AI-Supported Writing

Target time: 0:35

In the current PISA foreign-language assessment, we can compare writing without AI support and writing with AI support in a controlled environment. This is valuable because we have final products and process data. We can inspect how students write, how they interact with AI, and how process indicators relate to text quality.

Transition: The first result concerns score distributions.

## Slide 39. Texts Written with Chatbots Receive Higher Scores

Target time: 2:35

Across content, holistic quality, language quality, and organization, chatbot-supported texts shift toward higher scores. This does not yet tell us whether students learn more, but it shows that AI support changes the quality of produced texts. That is the starting point for more detailed process analysis.

Action: click the distribution plot to open the automated-scoring result.

The first popup shows that writing products can be scored reliably before students finish. Model performance rises quickly over writing time, and fusion models perform especially well early. That matters pedagogically because feedback is most useful while students can still revise.

Action: click `Writing activity`.

The keylogging result shows that texts are stronger when students type more. This is visible both without AI support and with AI support. The important point is that writing activity remains meaningful. Even in AI-supported writing, students' own interaction with the text is related to quality.

Action: click `Paste events`.

Here we see that texts are stronger when students paste in more generated text. The rank correlation between pasted insertions and text quality is rho equals .469, with p below .001. This does not mean that copying generated text is automatically good learning. It means that generated text insertions are part of the writing process and can be linked empirically to text quality.

Action: click `Argument coding`, and then click the plot inside that popup if you want to explain the coding dimensions.

In the argument-coding analysis, we inspect whether students argue with teacher-student relationships, teachers' subject knowledge, or both. Relationship arguments include belonging, individual support, motivation, role models, and safe learning environments. Knowledge arguments include subject mastery, clear explanations, trust, respect, and cognitive activation. This links process data with educational meaning: not only how much students write or paste, but what kinds of arguments they produce.
