## Source Identity

Jansen, Meyer, Hattie, and Moeller (2024) conduct a second-order meta-analytic review of correlates of self-beliefs in childhood and adolescence. The paper appears in *Psychological Bulletin* and synthesizes first-order meta-analyses rather than primary studies directly.

The central construct is **self-beliefs**: relatively enduring evaluative beliefs about oneself, including broad and domain-specific forms such as self-concept, self-esteem, and self-efficacy. The focal population is children and adolescents in K-12 education.

## What This Source Contributes

This paper is useful for the wiki because it gives a large-scale map of variables related to students' self-beliefs. It is especially relevant for questions about motivation, learning, achievement, student characteristics, intervention design, and how strongly different variables relate to students' views of themselves.

The study distinguishes two main evidence areas:

- **Personal characteristics**: observational associations between self-beliefs and achievement, cognitive ability, noncognitive variables, and background variables.
- **Interventions**: intervention effects on self-beliefs, including interventions targeting personal outcomes, self-beliefs directly, physical activity, psychological competencies, technology, school context, and teaching/classroom structure.

## Core Takeaway

Personal characteristics are more strongly related to K-12 students' self-beliefs than interventions are, but intervention effects are still substantial. Within personal characteristics, achievement and noncognitive variables have the strongest associations. Within interventions, those that directly target self-beliefs have the largest effects.

## Important Caution

The paper repeatedly warns that most evidence is correlational or aggregated from meta-analyses, so the results describe relative strength of associations, not causal direction. The ARA should therefore avoid language such as "causes" unless referring specifically to intervention evidence.

## Human-Readable Summary

The paper asks what is most strongly related to positive self-beliefs in children and adolescents. It reviews meta-analyses on self-beliefs during K-12 education and compares the strength of relations across large variable families. The strongest relations are with achievement and noncognitive variables such as emotions, motivation, perceived social support, other self-beliefs, and problematic technology use. Interventions can support self-beliefs, especially when they target self-beliefs directly, but their average effects are smaller than the observational associations for personal characteristics.

The paper also reviews the transparency and reproducibility of the underlying meta-analytic literature. It finds improving reporting quality over time, but also gaps: preregistration, published data, and statistical code are still uncommon. The paper therefore functions both as a content synthesis about self-beliefs and as a methodological map of the meta-analytic evidence base.
