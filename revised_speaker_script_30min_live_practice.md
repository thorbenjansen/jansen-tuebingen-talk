## Delivery Principle

The talk should not sound like a list of projects. The spine is:

I build and study effective teaching and learning arrangements for argumentation. The recurring problem is that argumentation is important but difficult to teach, assess, and support in real time. My contribution is to combine learning theory, formative assessment, automated analysis, motivation research, and scalable teacher support into a coherent research program.

The stronger framing is use-inspired basic research: I start from theory, build an intervention from that theory, test the intervention under increasingly realistic conditions, and return to theory development. The talk therefore moves from theory to intervention and back to theory.

## Slide 1. Effective Argumentation Instruction Across Science and Language Education

Target time: 0:30

Script:

Thank you very much for the opportunity to present my work. My talk is about effective argumentation instruction across science and language education. The central idea is that students learn argumentation best when we can make their thinking visible while it is still developing. This is where pedagogical AI becomes interesting: not as a replacement for teachers, but as a way to support formative assessment, motivation, and learning in the process.

Transition:

I will start with the educational problem that motivates the whole program.

## Slide 2. Starting Point

Target time: 1:05

Script:

My starting point is that argumentative competencies are central for participation in society. Students need to build positions, justify them with evidence, communicate them clearly, and revise them when they encounter better reasons. At the same time, argumentation is not one simple skill. In science education, students have to coordinate claims, evidence, models, and domain knowledge. In language education, they also have to organize a text, formulate a position, and produce linguistically appropriate arguments.

That creates a very practical problem for teaching. Effective argumentation instruction requires continuous diagnosis and feedback while students are working. Teachers need to see what students understand, where their argument breaks down, and what kind of support would help next. Digital tools and AI can help by making parts of this process visible and by supporting teachers and students before the final product is already finished.

Transition:

This connects directly to a broader framework of teaching quality.

## Slide 3. Teaching Quality

Target time: 0:55

Script:

I locate my work in the teaching-quality framework, especially the dimensions cognitive activation, student support, and classroom management. For argumentation, cognitive activation means prompting students to reason with evidence, claims, and quality criteria instead of only producing opinions. Student support means that feedback arrives at a time when students can still use it. Classroom management means that teachers can organize complex learning environments in which students write, revise, use tools, and receive guidance.

The phrase "pedagogical AI" is important here. I am not interested in AI as a generic technology. I am interested in AI when it is embedded in teaching arrangements that improve diagnosis, feedback, and learning.

Transition:

The structure of the talk follows the logic of developing such an intervention.

## Slide 4. Structure of the Talk

Target time: 0:40

Script:

I will structure the talk as use-inspired basic research. The logic is that I start from theory, build an intervention from that theory, test whether the intervention works, and then return to theory development.

First, I identify target-group needs. Second, I develop the intervention basis: formative assessment, learning progressions, and automated assessment. Third, I show pilot evidence. Fourth, I move to efficacy studies, where the focus is internal validity: under controlled conditions, does the mechanism work? Fifth, I move to effectiveness studies, where the focus is external validity: how does the mechanism work under more realistic instructional conditions? Sixth, I show how this line of work scales into professional development, larger projects, and grant acquisition. At the end, I return to theory and current work.

So the overall aim is not only to test isolated tools. The aim is to build evidence-based teaching and learning arrangements and use those arrangements to advance theory.

Transition:

So first: what are the needs of the target groups?

## Slide 5. Needs of a Target Group

Target time: 0:30

Script:

The first part maps the needs of teachers and students. I use three evidence sources. The first is work on teachers' professional competencies in assessment. The second is large-scale work on argumentative competencies in science and second-language writing. The third is meta-analytic work on learning-relevant outcomes, especially achievement, motivation, and self-beliefs.

Transition:

I begin with teachers, because their assessment competence is central for formative support.

## Slide 6. Teachers' Professional Competencies

Target time: 0:50

Script:

This slide summarizes my work on teachers' professional competencies and judgment accuracy. It connects to roughly 40 years of research on teacher judgment accuracy. The central idea in this tradition is simple but powerful: we compare teachers' judgments with students' correct or criterion-based performance and then ask where judgments are accurate, where they are biased, and under which conditions they improve.

Our contribution was to bring this logic into argumentative writing. That is demanding, because teachers do not simply "see" argumentative competence. Their judgments are shaped by teacher characteristics, student characteristics, task features, and the available evidence. We also found judgment biases: teachers can be influenced by cues that are not the central target of assessment, and this can distort how they evaluate students' arguments.

For a teacher, assessing an argument is especially difficult because content accuracy, structure, evidence use, reasoning, and language all interact. This matters because feedback quality depends on assessment quality. If the assessment is noisy or biased, the feedback will also be noisy or biased. My work shows that teacher judgment can be studied as a systematic competence and that it can be supported. This is one reason why assessment training and AI-supported tools become relevant later in the talk.

Transition:

The second need concerns students' argumentative competencies in authentic large-scale writing tasks.

## Slide 7. Large-Scale Studies About Argumentative Competencies

Target time: 0:55

Script:

Here I show two empirical foundations. In science, we analyzed 4,589 essays from 1,839 secondary students. The goal was to understand argumentation in German-language science essays, where students have to coordinate evidence and domain-specific explanations. In foreign-language argumentation, we worked with 2,314 TOEFL argumentative essays from year 11 students in Germany and Switzerland.

The important point is that argumentation cannot be assessed only in the abstract. It has to be validated in the tasks students actually write. These studies gave us corpora, rating criteria, and evidence about where students struggle: with connecting claims to evidence, organizing reasoning, and producing arguments that are convincing within a specific subject.

Transition:

The third evidence source broadens the view from performance to motivation and self-beliefs.

## Slide 8. Meta-Analyses About Learning-Relevant Outcomes

Target time: 0:35

Script:

These meta-analyses are important because intervention research should not focus only on achievement. Learning is also shaped by motivation and self-beliefs. In the meta-analyses I conducted on student motivation and self-concept, one result became especially important for this program: students' motivation and self-beliefs are strongly connected to whether they experience that they are learning. They need to see that what they do can improve their competence.

This also connects to expectancy-value theory. Students engage when they expect that they can succeed and when they see value in the task. But those expectations and values are not abstract. They are shaped by concrete learning experiences. If feedback helps students see progress, then feedback can affect not only performance, but also the motivational basis of further learning.

For this talk, the key implication is: feedback and AI support should be evaluated not only by whether a text improves, but also by whether students remain engaged, feel competent, and learn to regulate their own writing.

Transition:

That leads to the first interim conclusion.

## Slide 9. Interim Conclusion: Target-Group Needs Define the Intervention

Target time: 0:40

Script:

The conclusion from this first section is simple: target-group needs must define the intervention. Teachers are challenged to assess complex argumentation. Students are challenged to produce arguments that are coherent, evidence-based, and subject-specific. The risk is to design digital tools from a technology perspective rather than from an evidence-based needs perspective.

My contribution is to map those needs empirically and then use them to build support for teachers and students. The intervention logic is therefore not only to improve final products. It is to make learning visible so that students and teachers can see progress while learning is still taking place.

Transition:

The next section explains how this evidence is translated into intervention development.

## Slide 10. Intervention Development

Target time: 0:25

Script:

The development section has two foundations. The theoretical basis is formative assessment and learning progressions for argumentation. The methodological basis is automated assessment, because scalable feedback requires models that can analyze student texts with sufficient quality.

Transition:

I start with formative assessment.

## Slide 11. Formative Assessment

Target time: 0:35

Script:

Formative assessment is built around three student questions: Where am I going? Where am I now? And what is the next step? In feedback terms, this corresponds to feed-up, feedback, and feed-forward. For argumentation instruction, this is crucial because students need more than a score. They need to understand the goal, locate their current argument, and receive guidance for revision.

Transition:

But formative feedback also needs a model of how argumentation becomes more sophisticated.

## Slide 12. Learning Progression for Augmentation

Target time: 0:55

Script:

This slide shows the learning progression that organizes the intervention logic. The progression starts with a basic but essential skill: students first have to understand the context of the task and form an initial position. Without this, later feedback on argument quality has little value.

The second step is building specific arguments. Here students move from a claim, to claim plus evidence, and then to claim, evidence, and warrant. In other words, they do not only state what they think. They connect their position to evidence and explain why this evidence supports the position.

The third step is refinement. Students test the argument, defend it, evaluate alternatives, address counterarguments, and revise. This progression matters because feedback should not be generic. A student who has not understood the context needs different support from a student who has a claim but weak evidence, or from a student who already needs to address counterarguments. The progression gives us a theory-driven structure for assessment, feedback, and AI support.

Transition:

I illustrate this with the task level.

## Slide 13. Understanding the Task

Target time: 0:35

Script:

At the first level, students have to understand the task context and build a position. In this example, they decide which renewable energy converter a district should promote: wind, solar, or hydroelectric power. Even this step is not trivial. Students have to interpret the goal, compare options, and understand criteria such as energy output, climate impact, and reliability.

The initial position here is "promote a wind park." That is the starting point. But it is not yet an argument. It becomes useful for learning only when students can connect it to evidence and reasoning.

Transition:

The next step is turning that position into justification.

## Slide 14. Building the Argument

Target time: 0:55

Script:

Here the position becomes an argument. Students move from a simple claim, to claim plus evidence, and then to claim, evidence, and warrant. A stronger argument does not only say "I choose wind." It gives evidence, for example that the local wind potential is strong. It then adds the warrant: strong wind potential allows high energy output with low emissions, and therefore wind is the best fit under the task criteria.

The highest level also includes counterclaims and rebuttals. Students recognize that solar and hydro can help, but they explain why wind is still the most suitable option. This is the kind of structure that automated feedback should detect and support.

Transition:

The final step is refinement through interaction and revision.

## Slide 15. Refining the Argument

Target time: 0:30

Script:

At the refinement stage, students test their argument with others. They ask questions, defend their position, evaluate weaknesses, and revise. This is where argumentation becomes a learning process rather than a one-shot product. The goal is not only a better text, but a better understanding of why the argument is stronger.

Transition:

To support this at scale, we need reliable assessment data.

## Slide 16. Human Expert Ratings

Target time: 0:55

Script:

This slide shows the empirical basis for automated assessment. In science argumentation, we used learning-progression-based ratings that capture content and structure at the sentence level. In foreign-language argumentation, we used analytical and holistic expert ratings for content, style, organization, and language quality, including work with ETS.

The important methodological point is that the AI models are trained against high-quality human expert ratings. We do not ask the model to invent what quality means. We first define quality through theory and expert judgment, then test whether automated models can reproduce those judgments with sufficient reliability.

Transition:

That leads to the second interim conclusion.

## Slide 17. Interim Conclusion: AI Can Simulate Human Assessment of Argumentation

Target time: 0:35

Script:

The conclusion from the development section is that AI can accurately simulate human assessment of argumentation when the criteria are theoretically grounded and the human ratings are strong. This is important because it makes formative assessment scalable. It allows us to move from delayed summative correction to process-oriented feedback while students are still writing.

Transition:

The next question is whether this support works in practice.

## Slide 18. Pilot Study

Target time: 0:30

Script:

The pilot section tests the mechanism of action on both sides of the instructional process. On the teacher side, the question is whether tools can train assessment competencies by helping teachers focus on the relevant features of student arguments. On the student side, the question is whether AI-based feedback supports revision and learning.

This balance is important: the program is not only about student-facing AI. It is also about teacher professional competence. If teachers are supposed to support argumentation, then they need assessment tools and training that make the relevant qualities of an argument visible.

Transition:

I first show the teacher-side tool.

## Slide 19. TRACE Tool

Target time: 0:40

Script:

The TRACE Tool is a teacher assessment simulation. Teachers practice assessment decisions on student argumentation, receive structured cases, and can compare their judgments to expert-based criteria. The idea is similar to deliberate practice: teachers encounter diagnostic situations repeatedly and receive support for calibrating their judgments.

The mechanism of action is that the tool makes the relevant assessment points in the text salient. For example, it can direct attention to the quality of the first argument: whether there is a claim, whether the evidence actually supports it, and whether the reasoning connects both. At the same time, parts of the text that should not dominate the assessment become less salient. In this way, the tool supports diagnostic focus and reduces the influence of irrelevant cues.

This line of work matters for a professorship in teaching and learning because it connects teacher professionalization with evidence-based assessment.

Transition:

On the student side, the first question was whether AI feedback improves revision.

## Slide 20. Pilot Study Result: AI-Based Feedback Supports Revision

Target time: 0:55

Script:

Across pilot work, AI-based feedback supports revision. It can improve revision performance, it works better than no external feedback, and in some conditions the effects can be similar to teacher feedback. This does not mean that AI replaces teacher feedback. It means that feedback generated from automated assessment can become an additional support in situations where teachers cannot provide immediate individual feedback to every student.

For me, this is a crucial result: the technical system is only educationally relevant if students actually revise better and if the feedback can be embedded into instruction.

Transition:

But the evidence also shows a major challenge.

## Slide 21. Challenge: AI Feedback Does Not Help All Students Equally

Target time: 0:55

Script:

The challenge is that AI feedback does not help all students equally. In one large context, about half of 20,000 students did not use AI feedback. And even when feedback is available, effects are distributed unequally. Some students engage deeply, revise strategically, and benefit. Others ignore the feedback, misunderstand it, or do not transform it into revision.

This is the point where the research program moves beyond the simple question "does AI feedback work?" The more important question becomes: for which teachers and students, through which mechanisms, and under which instructional conditions does it work?

Transition:

That motivates the efficacy and effectiveness studies.

The student line is a feedback experiment in which we varied the structure of feedback based on Hattie and Timperley's feedback model.

The key question in this phase is whether the mechanism works under controlled conditions. Can teacher assessment be trained? Can feedback support revision? And what kind of feedback structure is necessary for students to benefit?

## New Slide 24. Efficacy Feedback Study: Combining Feed-Up, Feedback, and Feed-Forward

Target time: 0:45

Script:

In the student-side efficacy studies, we varied feedback following Hattie and Timperley's feedback model. The model distinguishes three questions. First: Where am I going? That is the target, or feed-up. Second: Where am I now? That is feedback on the current state. Third: What is the next step? That is feed-forward.

The important result is that isolated feedback is not enough. Students benefit most when all three questions are answered together. They need to understand the goal, locate their current performance, and know what to do next. This is the mechanism that turns feedback into usable learning support.

Transition:

The second grant extends this student-side work into classrooms.

## Slide 25. Interim Conclusion: Feedback Has to Become Learning Support

Target time: 0:40

Script:

At this point, the conclusion is that AI feedback can increase revision performance, but feedback alone is not enough. Feedback must become learning support. This requires the full cycle: students need a target, information about their current performance, and guidance for the next step.

Effects are also unequal, and all students should benefit. Therefore, the research focus shifts to uptake: how students perceive feedback, whether they engage with it, how they revise, and how this affects motivation and learning.

The effectiveness study is designed around exactly that mechanism.

## Slide 26. Effectiveness Study Procedure

Target time: 0:50

Script:

The effectiveness study moves from internal validity to external validity. Here, the question is how the intervention functions under more realistic instructional conditions. Students write, receive support, revise, and answer questionnaires. The procedure allows us to connect the feedback condition to process variables such as engagement and to outcomes such as text quality and motivation.

The design is important because it treats writing as a process. We are not only comparing final texts. We examine what happens between first draft, feedback, revision, and student perception. That is where learning support either succeeds or fails.

Transition:

The first result concerns engagement during revision.

## Slide 27. Main Effect: AI Feedback Increases Engagement During Text Revision

Target time: 0:55

Script:

The main effect is that AI feedback increases engagement during text revision. Students who receive generative-AI feedback engage more with argumentative elements during revision. This is educationally meaningful because revision is often the moment where feedback becomes learning. If students do not engage, feedback remains external information. If they engage, feedback can shape attention, strategy, and improvement.

This result supports the idea that AI feedback can change not only the product but also the process of writing.

Transition:

But the challenge remains: which students benefit most?

## Slide 28. Research Question

Target time: 0:40

Script:

The moderation question is: Which student characteristics change how generative-AI feedback affects writing? In the model, the feedback condition is generative-AI feedback versus self-feedback. The outcomes are writing quality and motivation. Student characteristics are the moderators, and engagement during revision is the central mechanism we inspect.

This question matters because equal access to AI feedback does not guarantee equal benefit. We need to know which characteristics shape uptake, which students engage with feedback, and where instruction has to compensate.

Transition:

The result can be summarized as a mechanism through engagement.

## Slide 29. Student Characteristics Moderate the Effect of AI Feedback via Engagement

Target time: 1:05

Script:

The central result is that student characteristics moderate the effect of AI feedback through engagement. In other words, feedback does not act directly on learning in a simple way. Students first have to perceive it as useful, engage with it, and transform it into revision behavior. Their receptivity, motivation, and background influence this process.

This is the conceptual bridge between feedback research and motivation research. AI feedback is not only an information intervention. It is part of a motivational and self-regulatory process. Students differ in whether they trust the feedback, whether they feel capable of using it, and whether they invest effort in revision. That is why the same feedback can lead to different outcomes.

## Slide 35. Theory

Target time: 1:00

Script:

The theoretical contribution has three parts. First, the work integrates AI into motivation and self-belief formation. Students compare themselves not only with peers or standards, but increasingly with AI-supported performances and AI-generated suggestions. Second, the work expands argumentation learning progressions with AI support. Students can build, inspect, and revise arguments with AI as part of the learning environment. Third, it expands formative assessment theory through process feedback. Feedback is not only a message after performance; it becomes a sequence of uptake, engagement, revision, and motivational consequences.

This is why I see the program as more than applied AI research. It contributes to theory about how teaching, feedback, motivation, and learning change when AI enters the process.

## Slide 36. Challenge: Students Use Generative AI While Writing

Target time: 0:45

Script:

The current challenge is that students already use generative AI while writing. They can ask for ideas, formulations, structure, and revision support. This changes the learning environment and creates a new assessment challenge. We need to understand what students actually do with AI, how this affects argumentation, and how it changes motivation and learning.

For me, this is not a reason to avoid AI in education. It is a reason to study it carefully and design better instructional arrangements.

## Slide 37. PISA Gives a Controlled Window Into AI-Supported Writing

Target time: 1:00

Script:

In the current PISA foreign-language assessment, we can compare writing without AI support and writing with AI support in a controlled environment. The sample includes 1,699 students from Germany, age 15. Each student writes two texts, 30 minutes each: one without AI support and one with AI support.

This is valuable because we do not only have final products. We also have process data. That means we can inspect how students write, how they interact with AI, and how process indicators relate to text quality. In a field where AI use is often discussed abstractly, this gives us behavioral evidence.

Transition:

The first process signal is typed text.

## Slide 38. Texts Are Stronger When Students Type More

Target time: 0:45

Script:

The keylogging data show that texts are stronger when students type more. This is visible both without AI support and with AI support, although the patterns differ. The important point is that writing activity remains meaningful. Even in AI-supported writing, students' own interaction with the text is related to quality.

This helps avoid a simplistic view where AI replaces student effort. The process data show that effort, production, and engagement still matter.

Transition:

The second process signal concerns pasted generated text.

## Slide 39. Texts Are Stronger When Students Paste in More Generated Text

Target time: 0:45

Script:

Here we see that texts are stronger when students paste in more generated text. The rank correlation between pasted insertions and text quality is rho equals .469, with p below .001. This does not mean that copying generated text is automatically good learning. It means that generated text insertions are part of the writing process and can be empirically linked to text quality.

The next question is interpretive: are students using AI as a shortcut, as a scaffold, or as material they transform?

Transition:

That is why we also code the arguments inside the texts.

## Slide 40. Coding Students' Arguments in Their Texts

Target time: 0:45

Script:

In this analysis, we inspect whether students argue with teacher-student relationships, teachers' subject knowledge, or both. The goal is to move beyond surface-level AI use and look at the content of students' reasoning. For example, do they argue that good teaching depends on care, belonging, motivation, and a safe environment? Or do they argue that it depends on subject mastery, clear explanations, and critical thinking?

This links automated process data with educational meaning: not only how much students write or paste, but what kinds of arguments they produce.

## Slide 42. The Multistage Feedback Uptake Model

Target time: 0:50

Script:

The Multistage Feedback Uptake Model summarizes the central mechanism. Feedback can be lost at several points: students may not notice it, may not understand it, may not trust it, may not engage with it, or may not transform it into revision. But when uptake succeeds, feedback becomes part of a chain that links information, perception, engagement, revision, motivation, and learning.

This is the future direction of my work: to build theory-based, evidence-based, and scalable teaching arrangements in which AI supports teachers and students without replacing the pedagogical core of teaching.

Closing sentence:

In short, my research asks how we can make learning visible in the process and use that visibility to improve argumentation, motivation, and learning. Thank you very much.
